Spiritism

By Lauren McLaughlin

Spiritism was a nineteenth-century religious-philosophical and quasi-scientific movement centered on communication with the spirits of the dead. Its practices included séances, table-turning, and other attempts to make unseen spiritual forces physically reachable. Although Spiritism overlapped with religion due to its concern for the soul, the afterlife, and the spiritual realm, it went far beyond traditional Christianity. Kardec’s system incorporated Christian moral language, but it also revised — and sometimes rejected — major Christian ideas like heaven and the Trinity, simultaneously emphasizing ideas like reincarnation and mediumship (Encyclopedia.com).

Allan Kardec’s The Spirits’ Book — first published in 1857 — became one of the foundational texts of Spiritism and helped define the movement as a “doctrine” based on messages communicated through mediums (Britannica). Kardec’s work also gave Spiritism a more structured intellectual form. Rather than presenting mediumship as a superstition, Kardec described Spiritism as a “science” concerned with spirits and their relation to the physical world (Gulão et al. 5). This distinction plays a distinct role in Leo Tolstoy’s Anna Karenina. Tolstoy does not use Spiritism to represent genuine religious faith but as a fashionable attempt to make these practices appear academic and scientific.

Spiritism emerged in the wider European context of nineteenth-century fascination with science and invisible forces. By the 1850s, table-turning had become a popular practice in Europe, and Kardec himself encountered the practice through reports of tables autonomously moving to reveal coded messages (Gulão et al. 4). Table-turning was not a primarily religious ritual but a source of amusement and a supposed research topic. Melvyn Willin describes it as “part parlour amusement, part spiritual technology and part research problem,” noting that it took place in both households and laboratories (Willin). Spiritualists often employed scientific language, even comparing spiritual forces to electrical forces and presenting séances as experiments. Kardec’s own method encouraged this mixture of science and spiritual beliefs. He argued that Spiritism relied on observation and experience, and later writers note that he compared mediums to tools like microscopes and telescopes, suggesting that the spiritual realm is like another palpable realm that could be studied with the proper tools (Gulão et al. 12).

This context informs Vronsky’s defense — and Levin’s criticism — of table-turning in Anna Karenina. When Vronsky compares Spiritism to electricity, he uses contemporary, fashionable language to make Spiritism sound credible. Tolstoy’s disapproval of Spiritism did not stem from its being supernatural. Rather, he objected to its intellectual dishonesty and social superficiality. Through Levin, Tolstoy presents Spiritism as a symptom of the educated class’s arrogance. During a dinner at the Shcherbatsky’s household, momentarily after Kitty rejected Levin’s proposal and when Vronsky and Levin meet for the first time, Levin says that table-turning proves “that the so-called educated class is in no way superior to the peasants” (Tolstoy 54). Levin provides a glimpse into Tolstoy’s feelings, adding a second layer, criticizing the belief that educated spiritualists are smarter than peasants. Thus, Spiritism allows the educated elite to indulge in irrational concepts under the guise of serious scientific inquiry.

In Levin’s strongest criticism of Spiritism, he distinguishes scientific investigation from spiritualist indulgences. He argues that spiritualists began with “tables writing to them and spirits visiting them,” and only afterward claimed that these events were caused by an “unknown force” (Tolstoy 55). In other words, they start with a supernatural conclusion and then forge a scientific explanation. For Tolstoy, this idea reverses the proper order of events; a scientific explanation should arise from careful observation, but Spiritism begins with an exciting spectacle and invents a theory to justify it.

Later in the novel, Landau, a French medium-like figure, shows that Spiritism is not necessarily harmless. In Part 7, Countess Lydia Ivanovna invites Landau to Karenin’s home to inform his decision on whether he should divorce Anna. Landau enters a trance-like state and delivers pronouncements that the Countess and Karenin treat as authoritative and instructive. Once Landau’s statements are interpreted by the Countess and Karenin as disapproval of the divorce, Oblonsky has failed in his mission to secure Karenin’s compliance (Tolstoy, Part 7, Chapter 22). For Tolstoy, therefore, Spiritism can be dangerously substituted for moral judgment.

Spiritualism, therefore, should not be confused with Levin’s religious awakening. At the end of the novel, Levin’s faith comes from wisdom, humility, and a modest life. Therefore, the nature of his awakening sharply diverges from the spiritualists. Levin’s faith does not depend on séances or mediums but on life experience.

Overall, Spiritism in Anna Karenina appears as part of Moscow’s social atmosphere. In even the brief conversation between Levin and Vronsky at the Sherbatsky’s home, a major tension between characters emerges. Vronsky treats the concept of table-turning as potentially credible. Countess Nordstrom uses the conversation to tease Levin, and Kitty is caught between her emotional connection to Levin and Vronsky’s status and attractiveness. Meanwhile, Levin becomes isolated because he takes the issue more seriously. In addition to discussing Spiritism, therefore, the topic dramatizes Levin’s discomfort in Russian high society. Through both the early conversation about table-turning and the Landau scene, Tolstoy presents Spiritism as a superficial practice that exposes the Russian elites’ hunger for novelty and authority.
