Zemstvo Reform and Local Self-Government

By Anna Wetzel

The Zemstvo was a form of local self-government instituted in the Russian Empire during the Great Reforms of Alexander II. The Zemstvos were first introduced in 1864 with the enactment of the Zemstvo Statute. The legislation was produced by the Commission on the Reorganization of Provincial and District Institutions, authorized by the Tsar in 1859 in anticipation of the emancipation of the serfs in 1861 and the resulting “crisis of under-government” in the provinces (Lincoln 90). According to the design of the 1864 Zemstvo Statute, the Zemstvos would be elected as district and provincial assemblies — independent of the central imperial bureaucracy — tasked with managing local economic, social, and political needs in the Russian provinces. The Zemstvos covered a wide range of activities: “building roads and maintaining rural post offices, organizing health care and agronomic assistance, public education, affordable insurance, and economic incentives for small producers” (Gerasimov 177). The Zemstvos were highly effective, and they carried out the tasks of public administration more efficiently than the central government did. The Zemstvo reforms were met with extreme enthusiasm from Russian society, who were excited to be represented in local government by elected, all-estate assemblies. The Zemstvos were created not only to solve the practical problems of local government, but to politically mobilize the nation by including them in public self-government. Despite the popular appeal of the Zemstvo’s revolutionary appearance, the assemblies were not populist in their demographic makeup. Due to a weighted voting system, the landowning nobility maintained a dominant influence. Furthermore, the Zemstvos did not act as a counter-balance to the powers of the centralized bureaucracy; the only power the Zemstvos possessed was that granted to them by imperial decree. Indeed, the Zemstvos were vulnerable to the whims of the imperial government; in 1890, Alexander III reformed the Zemstvo so that the nobility had an even greater influence and the peasantry serving on the Zemstvo boards were no longer elected, but appointed by the governor. Despite the restrictions put on them by the Tsar and imperial bureaucracy, the Zemstvos’ political role and importance grew over time. By the eve of World War I, the Zemstvos were more important and popular than the central government. Historians generally view the Zemstvo’s role in local government as practically effective, filling the “giant void” in subprovincial administration (Gerasimov 178; though it should be noted that historians’ assessment of the emancipation reform is generally negative). However, scholars agree that the popular idea propagated by Zemstvo leaders and members of the liberal intelligentsia that the Zemstvos were genuinely independent, populist assemblies is pure mythology. Tolstoy viewed the Zemstvos with skepticism, as he viewed most efforts at governmental reform formulated as a distance from immediate, case-based knowledge. As a landowner, Tolstoy felt the best way to manage the land and the peasants who labored on it was through direct contact and knowledge, rather than the mediated, artificial proceedings of local bureaucracies and lofty, imported ideals of the reform movement. Tolstoy’s view of the Zemstvo reforms in Anna Karenina is mainly conveyed through Levin. Levin had formerly served on a Zemstvo board, but quit due to his disillusionment with the local government. When his half-brother Koznyshev asked why he had quit, Levin responded: “Well, to put it bluntly, I became convinced that there is no zemstvo activity, nor can there be,’... it is a way for the local coterie to make a bit of money. It used to be trustees and courts, and now we have the zemstvo…not in the form of bribes, but unearned salaries” (Tolstoy 21). Levin viewed the Zemstvo as an opportunity for noblemen to enrich themselves, rather than a genuine forum for improving the lives of landowners and peasants. Moreover, Tolstoy was generally skeptical of applying general, foreign templates to particular circumstances in Russia, and the Zemstvo reforms were closely associated with Europeanness. For instance, the influential Prince Alexander Vasilchikov authored a three-volume treatise (written between 1869 and 1871) entitled, On Self-Government: A Comparative Review of Russian and Foreign Zemstvo, comparing the British, French, and German traditions of local government with Russia’s new Zemstvo institutions. The Zemstvo Statute and subsequent reforms seemed to aspire to a certain Europeanness, at least in the public consciousness of the late 19th century. Tolstoy saw abstract, imported solutions as ill-suited to the problems and demands of local circumstances, a further explanation for his skepticism towards the Zemstvo reforms.
