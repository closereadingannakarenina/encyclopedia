Marriage and Divorce Laws in Imperial Russia

By Sibora Pllumbaj

In Imperial Russia, marriage was both a religious and legal institution governed primarily by The Orthodox Church rather than by civil courts. Divorce therefore fell under ecclesiastical jurisdiction and could only be granted on limited legal grounds officially recognized by Church authorities. As Gregory Freeze explains in “Profane Narratives about a Holy Sacrament,” the officially recognized grounds for divorce included permanent disappearance without communication, adultery, Siberian exile accompanied by loss of all civil rights, premarital impotence, premarital insanity, bigamy, and several lesser grounds (Freeze 148). Early divorce cases were often connected to bigamy or Siberian exile, while later proceedings increasingly centered on adultery, which gradually became the most common basis for divorce because it could be formally demonstrated within court proceedings.

The divorce procedure itself was highly structured and involved multiple stages of ecclesiastical review. The process began with a formal petition (proshenie), followed by an attempted reconciliation (primirenie), during which the local priest encouraged the couple to preserve the marriage. If reconciliation failed, the case proceeded to a formal hearing (sudogovorenie), involving sworn depositions from eyewitnesses, investigation of evidence, and examination of testimony. The court then compiled the case summary (zapiska), signed by both parties, before preparing a final report for the bishop. If approved, the case was forwarded to the Holy Synod for final confirmation (Freeze 147–153). Because the Church required formally demonstrable guilt, many proceedings depended heavily on carefully coordinated testimony and procedural performance. As Freeze notes, cases often involved “half-truths and outright lies” (Freeze 151).

At the same time, Freeze emphasizes that divorce functioned very differently across social classes. Among elites, “the slick narrative of the educated ... ensured prompt divorce” (Freeze 153). Aristocratic families possessed the legal knowledge, social influence, and financial resources necessary to navigate the complicated process, sometimes arranging coordinated confessions of adultery in order to secure remarriage for the guilty party. Famous figures such as Prince Baryatinsky in Russia were able to arrange divorces by strategically assigning guilt and arranging a mutual consent divorce. The fact that Tsar Alexander II himself maintained a second family further exposed the gap between official doctrine and lived reality.

By contrast, divorce proved “infinitely more complicated” for disadvantaged groups, who often struggled with paperwork, procedural requirements, and lack of resources (Freeze 155). This distinction between privileged and disadvantaged divorce narratives is particularly important in understanding Anna Karenina, since the Karenins belonged precisely to the elite social world most capable of obtaining a divorce through cooperation and influence.

Tolstoy incorporates many aspects of this historical legal framework into Anna Karenina, while also reshaping certain elements of the divorce process for literary and psychological purposes. In Part 4, the lawyer explains that divorce could occur only under specific legal grounds, including “physical defects of the married partners; separation for five years without communication ... and adultery” (Tolstoy 371). The lawyer’s insistence that “he who wants the results must accept the means” (Tolstoy 372) reflects the procedural manipulation and coordinated guilt described by Freeze. Tolstoy’s portrayal of the lawyer himself also contributes to this critique of the system. His distracted behavior, including his fascination with catching moths during the consultation, and his visible satisfaction at the prospect of profiting from Karenin’s case, make the legal process appear mechanical and self-interested rather than morally elevated. It is also important to remember that judicial reforms were very recent in Russia at that time, which explains the system's multiple flaws.

At the same time, Tolstoy exaggerates the emotional impossibility of divorce beyond the historical reality described in Freeze’s account. As Morson argues, the Karenins’ failure to divorce does not stem entirely from legal barriers. Karenin initially agrees to grant Anna a Baryatinsky-style divorce, yet Anna “refuses his offer of a divorce ... out of pride” (Morson 116). Morson further writes that, “in a Dostoevskian way,” Anna resents Karenin “for his very goodness” (Morson 115). Because the Karenins belonged to the educated elite described by Freeze, the novel suggests that divorce may have remained possible earlier in the process had both parties cooperated. Rather than presenting divorce as legally impossible, Tolstoy transforms it into a psychological and emotional tragedy shaped by the inability of the characters to reconcile their conflicting desires.

Anna Karenina presents divorce law not simply as a rigid legal structure, but as a system shaped by class, cooperation, social pressure, and emotions. While Tolstoy draws heavily from the historical realities described by Freeze, he also amplifies the emotional and psychological dimensions of divorce for literary effect. The novel therefore transforms a historically possible legal process into a broader tragedy driven by pride, resentment, and failed timing between the characters themselves.
