Women's Emancipation

Related Articles

  • The Woman Question in 19th-Century Russia

The concept of women’s emancipation refers to the 19th-century, European-wide phenomenon of women’s expanded civic rights, education, and financial independence, as well as the philosophical discourses related to this phenomenon. In Anna Karenina, Tolstoy engages with the concept of women’s emancipation polemically; he is critical of the movement, especially its most radical manifestations. Although Russian writers began discussing women’s emancipation in the 1830s, Tolstoy in Anna Karenina most clearly appears to engage with the discourse on the “woman question” of the 1860s, with authors like Nikolai Dobrolyubov and Nikolai Chernyshevsky. The animating concept of these more radical figures (often categorized as nihilists) was the “emancipation of the person,” from all arbitrary authority, including “near-absolute authority” of husbands and fathers (Engels 60). Tolstoy did not view the family unit and traditional gender roles as oppressive or tyrannical; on the contrary, Tolstoy’s literature conveys the message that women reach their highest potential as dutiful wives and mothers.

The clearest evidence of Tolstoy’s attitude towards the “woman question” is his treatment of Anna’s plot arc. Anna chooses passion over family duties; she leaves her husband and son in order to pursue a love affair with Vronsky. But even after she bears Vronsky’s child, she defers the child-rearing to a team of nurses who breastfeed, care for, and acquaint themselves with the child in place of Anna. All of Anna’s efforts to fulfill herself outside of maternity fail; her attempts to write a book, aid in Vronsky’s philanthropic endeavours, and educate an English orphan are all aborted, and the affair that drove her to leave behind her family ends in rejection and heartbreak. Meanwhile, Kitty is peacefully married to Levin, and by the end of the novel, has borne his first child, whom she breastfeeds and knows intimately.

Anna’s failure to find fulfillment in family and motherhood ends in tragedy, and in more subtle ways, so do the trajectories of other childless women in the novel. As Goscilo writes in her essay on the subject, “For Tolstoy, marriage is a prelude to family, the optimal unit for human existence and social organization, faute de mieux, inasmuch as the single state eventually conduces to desiccation (Varenka, Koznyshev), childlessness results in the stasis of frivolity, ... while full-time engagement in sociopolitical and religious causes leads to patina and falsity (Mme Stahl, Lydia Ivanovna)” (Goscilo 87). Varenka’s life as a childless, but occupied nurse, elicits pitiful concern from Kitty. Madame Stahl and Lydia Ivanovna inspire disdain from many of the characters, including Kitty and her family. There is no fulfillment for the single or childless woman, Tolstoy makes clear, and no employment or extra-familial purpose can replace the duties of wife and mother.

Tolstoy most explicitly engages the discourse around the woman’s question through a conversation held at Oblonsky’s dinner (Part 4, Chapter 10). At the dinner party, Sergei and Pestsov, both fashionable Moscow intellectuals, engage in high-minded socio-political discussions with Karenin, while Oblonsky, Turgenevov, and Dolly occasionally interject. Pestsov eventually raises the “woman’s question,” to which he responds in favor of women’s emancipation. He argues that women are owed the education, rights, and duties of men; the difference between men and women is not their mental fitness, but women’s long subjugation. Karenin disagrees—he is skeptical that women are qualified to take on the civic duties entrusted to men, and fearing that education will inevitably lead to women’s assimilation into civil life, he argues against women’s education. Prince Shcherbatsky, in turn, agrees with Karenin that women are essentially ill-suited to civic duties. He analogizes the desire of a woman to pursue an education and profession to a man seeking a role as a wet nurse. The difference between men and women is essential, in his view, and women are simply less intelligent than their male peers.

In Engel's monograph, she argues that Tolstoy—like Dostoevsky in The Idiot—mocks the more extreme manifestations of women’s emancipation and conflates the unreasonable premise that women and men are in all respects interchangeable with the more reasonable argument that women and men should have equal political rights. However, we need not assume that Tolstoy is voicing his view on the woman question through Prince Shcherbatsky. Rather, the scene taken in its entirety and alongside the subsequent scene is more illustrative of Tolstoy’s view. While the men are having the discussion on women’s emancipation, Levin and Kitty are in the other room, not participating in the conversation. Levin finds their high-minded political musings useless, when compared to his preoccupation with Kitty, and his plan to propose marriage. Kitty is also disinterested, although she reflects that at another time she may have weighed on the conversation, but her feeling of anticipation regarding her burgeoning relationship with Levin completely occupies her attention. The sequence of the conversation on women’s emancipation and the marriage proposal reveal Tolstoy’s attitude toward the woman’s question.
