Faith

By Marie Wheeler

Faith is a constant theme throughout Anna Karenina, particularly in regards to Levin’s pondering and search for meaning. As noted by many scholars, Levin is a semi-autobiographical stand-in for Tolstoy, and therefore to understand Levin’s thoughts and critiques of faith, it is necessary to know how Tolstoy felt about and experienced faith throughout his life. While Tolstoy felt an instinctive religiosity from childhood, it was his conversion in the 1870s that induced his religious fervor. In the early 1870s, Tolstoy was corresponding with Strakhov, a Russian philosopher, and these exchanges cemented his search for meaning (Medzhibovskaya 161). Then, upon reading Pascal and Hume’s essays on religion, Tolstoy wrote that he found it impossible to believe in prayer; instead, he saw himself as a searcher whom God was concealed from, writing in chapter 12 of A Confession: “He knows and He sees my search, my struggle, and my grief. He does exist, I told myself” (Tolstoy 44). However, Tolstoy still wanted to apprehend God, and searched for other ways to communicate in order to provoke a response (Medzhibovskaya 171). Despite these struggles, Tolstoy briefly began an intense practice of the Orthodox religion from 1877 to 1878, and around the same time attempted to write his own catechism of faith, though it was left unfinished (Medzhibovskaya 174). However, this experimentation ended in a rupture, due to the church failing to live up to Tolstoy’s moral standards. Intersecting with this period of time was the writing of Anna Karenina, and therefore the struggle with faith played a prominent role in it.

Many characters in the novel reveal Tolstoy’s thoughts about faith and religion that he sees in society and is currently struggling through. Levin’s character is also searching for meaning as Tolstoy does. Throughout the book Levin has internal monologues, thinking about and analyzing everything while constantly switching between states of mind and arguing with other characters about differences in opinions. Meanwhile, the Orthodox-patriotic Lydia Ivanovna is shaped by Tolstoy’s contempt towards the real-life Lord Radstock, an evangelical preacher that high-society was swept up in. Tolstoy further reveals his disdain for false spirituality through Madame Stahl and her social circle, whom Kitty meets during her time abroad. Furthermore, throughout the novel the characters express varying responses to the “evil force” in their mind and will: Anna commits suicide in contrast to Levin’s final deep commitment to faith, Kitty rejects Varenka’s form of religion and accepts Levin’s struggles, and Vronsky never becomes a free moral agent (Medzhibovskaya 178). While each character demonstrates a small piece of Tolstoy’s current thoughts about faith and religion, it is Levin’s revelation in Part 8 that tied everything together. Here, a peasant informs Levin about a righteous old man who “lives for his soul. Remembers God” (Tolstoy 798). This moment strikes Levin and brings him to the culminating understanding that in life one should live to do God’s will and good for others. Tolstoy ends the novel on this message that life is no longer meaningless but “has the indisputable meaning of goodness” (Tolstoy 822).
