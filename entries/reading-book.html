Reading (a book)

By Sarah Jeon

In Leo Tolstoy’s Anna Karenina, the motif of reading is a tool to explore the limits of intellectualism and the disconnect between abstract knowledge and genuine understanding. Characters throughout the novel are repeatedly shown reading. However, Tolstoy consistently questions not whether his characters read — he questions what they read, how they read, and whether the books they read are helping them to engage meaningfully with reality. In other words, Tolstoy attempts to depict not necessarily merely the act of reading as significant, but whether or not reading brings his characters a genuine understanding of the world.

Levin’s relationship with reading demonstrates this tension most clearly. He is depicted reading books of agricultural theory, philosophy, and political writing. However, despite this, Tolstoy repeatedly shows that these books fail to resolve Levin’s deepest concerns. Reading leaves Levin frustrated and dissatisfied because theoretical agricultural books he reads do not account for the realities of Russian peasant labor. Philosophical texts similarly fail to satisfy his spiritual and religious qualms. However, rather than presenting Levin as someone who successfully combines intellectual reading with practical wisdom, Tolstoy depicts him as a character who over the course of the novel realizes how insufficient mere knowledge alone is. For instance, Levin’s ultimate spiritual revelation is not shaped by reading a book. It is from the lived experience of listening to the peasant Fyodor’s simple expression of faith. This choice by Tolstoy suggests that genuine wisdom in life cannot be fully achieved through mere intellectual study.

Anna’s relationship with reading is also significant. When on the train to St. Petersburg, she attempts to read an English novel but is unable to concentrate because she keeps thinking about Vronsky. Through Anna reading without absorbing anything, Tolstoy highlights the growing instability of her inner life. The fact that she is reading a fashionable “English novel” is also important in that it reflects a sort of shallowness. Later in Italy, Anna again attempts to read but cannot focus again because of her fixation on Vronsky. Her obsessive jealousy and emotional turmoil make it so that she cannot focus on reading. This repeated inability to read meaningfully reflects how Anna is so trapped within her own thoughts to the point of being incapable of engaging with external narratives. Tolstoy further complicates this motif of reading through characters such as Koznyshev, Metrov, and Katavasov. While these characters are all highly educated and very well-read, they are not portrayed as particularly wise by Tolstoy. Their contributions to conversations are primarily abstract theories, intellectual debates, and mere philosophical speculations. For example, while they may possess academic knowledge, they are disconnected from emotional and spiritual realities that Levin is shown to repeatedly try grappling with. Thus through these ‘learned’ characters, Tolstoy critiques intellectual culture. Karenin is also another important example. Even though he is one of the most intellectual readers in the novel, studying various texts, Karenin approaches reading as he approaches life: without depth. He reads very systematically and formally. Additionally, his intellectual habits reinforce his dependence on rules and structural order rather than a sense of deep self-awareness. Thus, despite Karenin’s extensive reading, it does not make him morally admirable or emotionally insightful. Instead, Tolstoy highlights the limits of a purely rational mentality. Additionally, Vronsky’s absence of reading is significant. Unlike the intellectual characters who immerse themselves fully in theory, Vronsky depends entirely on action and social performance. Thus, his lack of reading reflects that he favors immediate experience over introspection. Ultimately, Tolstoy uses the motif of reading to critique the idea that it is intellectual culture alone that can produce wisdom or morality. Across the novel, books repeatedly fail to provide characters with answers they individually seek. Whether through Levin’s dissatisfaction with philosophy, Anna’s inability to concentrate, Karenin’s very bureaucratic reading habits, how reading is of no assistance to Levin at all or the shallow intellectualism of ‘learned’ characters, Tolstoy implies that genuine understanding and wisdom in life come not from mere abstract theories. They come from direct human experience.
