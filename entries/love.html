Love

By Sarah Jeon

In Anna Karenina, Tolstoy defines love not as a stable, singular, or constantly romantic emotion. Rather, it is a multi-dimensional force, with layers of societal expectations, morality, and internal contradictions. The parallel plots of this novel allow Tolstoy to contrast the significance of love for Anna and Vronsky effectively, and for Levin and Kitty. Minor pairings like Oblonsky and Dolly or Varenka and Sergey Ivanovich also illuminate these different forms of love. For Kitty and Levin, love is genuine and intimate, rooted in a deep understanding and connection that transcends superficial attraction. Love here is presented as a beautiful thing that can connect two different people. It allows for genuine connection — such as when Levin was able to reveal to Kitty his lack of religious piety and his past sexual history — that is very intimate. Their marriage reflects Tolstoy’s ideal of a family dynamic: love is not merely about romantic feelings but can bring beneficial change to you as a person.

The significance of this scene is not simply in that it requires such emotional intimacy, but also that it requires so much trust and openness.

This ideal is reflected in Rousseau's Emile, or On Education. As one of Tolstoy’s favorite authors and an influential figure in his personal views, Rousseau served as a source for Tolstoy’s understanding of love. On Education argues that the domestic sphere preserves the integrity of the "natural man" against the corrupting forces of society. Tolstoy adopts this framework: the stable love of Kitty and Levin's marriage reflects a connection to the "natural order" of a family structure.

By contrast, for Anna and Vronsky, love is portrayed as driven by intense feelings and passion. It is not lacking in romantic love but rather lacks this ideal, stable family framework. In other words, they lack not love itself but rather the means to integrate romantic feelings into a stable domestic lifestyle.

Rather than the two of them working to build a shared domestic life, Anna and Vronsky both turn outward. During their stay in Italy in Europe in Part V, Vronsky has this sudden commitment to art — such as an intense desire to paint a portrait of Ann a— that is very performative. He lacks a genuine artistic vocation compared to Mikhailov, an actual professional. Thus, Vronsky’s artistic ambition is merely a temporary external hobby. As for Anna, she develops this obsessive fear that Vronsky does not love her enough. Particularly in Part VII, Chapters 30–31, she is so absorbed by her jealousy and suspicion of other women that she decides Vronsky is having an affair. This sort of displacement reveals the fragility of a relationship that departs from a traditional family unit and is based solely on passion.

Another important example is when Anna takes preventive measures to avoid getting pregnant to maintain her beauty and allure for Vronsky. At the same time, he dreams of a family for them. These two examples illustrate that even when two characters profess to and are depicted as being in love with each other, they may not genuinely love each other. Tolstoy depicts a severe lack of true, emotional intimacy and connection between Anna and Vronsky, in contrast to the more intimate love that Kitty and Levin have for each other. While Kitty and Levin, as lovers, can get through challenges (like Levin’s slight crush on Anna, Velovsky) together through communication, Anna and Vronsky’s love is built on secrecy.

Tolstoy also develops the idea of superficial infatuation versus genuine love by portraying Kitty’s experience at the ball in vivid detail, where love is depicted as a painful collapse of romantic illusions. At the beginning of the evening, Kitty feels admired and confident in her future with Vronsky. She feels encouraged by her mother and by society at large to be confident in her match with Vronsky. It is important to note that though Kitty is, in fact, flattered by Vronsky’s charm and attention to her, Tolstoy suggests that his feelings for him are rooted more in naive fascination and a feeling of societal expectations. Tolstoy does not depict a deep emotional connection between Kitty and Vronsky. This is very different from how he depicts Kitty’s initial relationship with Levin as more sincere and meaningful — even when she does not acknowledge it. However, her understanding and experience of love changed after observing Anna and Vronsky together. Vronsky choosing Anna for the mazurka instead of Kitty is a public display of his preference for Anna. It is also a stark example of his shallow affection for Kitty. By the end of the ball, Kitty can still be in love with and yet feel pain because of the one she loves: Vronsky. Here, Tolstoy portrays love as emotionally nuanced and even a bit naive or irrational. This scene at the ball serves to prime Kitty for a later, more emotionally significant, and mature experience of love with Levin.

Ultimately, Tolstoy presents love as a central mechanism for testing and revealing aspects of his characters. Genuine love — exemplified by Kitty and Levin — requires a resemblance to Tolstoy’s ideal family structure. It requires a spiritual morality and truth. Sexual, passionate love — as experienced by Anna and Vronsky — may not lack love, but without the structural stability of an agape love, it will be destructive and catastrophic. Tolstoy frames the concept of love as something whose value depends on the framework in which it operates.
