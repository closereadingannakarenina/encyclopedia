Meaning of Life

By Harold Campusano

The meaning of life is a key concept in Tolstoy’s Anna Karenina, which many of the novel's protagonists grapple with. Tolstoy himself, during his lifetime, was battling with this concept; he was particularly concerned with answering one of Kant’s three questions: “What can we hope for?” Thus, deconstructing the different ways in which Tolstoy engages with the meaning of life in Anna Karenina can help us better understand his personal perspective on the matter.

In the late 1870s, after having gained great literary esteem for works such as War and Peace (1869) and Anna Karenina (1877), Tolstoy underwent a moral crisis that would change his life forever. In 1876, Tolstoy’s depression and thoughts of suicide turned him towards religion in his search for life’s meaning. For instance, Tolstoy was intrigued by Pascal’s essays on “the silent and invisible presence of divinity in the world” (Medzhibovskaya 168). This influenced Tolstoy to begin searching for God (whom Pascal suggested was concealed) in various places, such as “forest clearings, in churches, within the family, at his writing desk…” (Medzhibovskaya 168). In a letter to his sister, Alexandrine, Tolstoy confessed that he had “stopped believing in philosophy,” but was still struggling to believe in prayer wholeheartedly (Medzhibovskaya 168). While surrounding himself with “pilgrims, monks and peasants, orthodox Christians as well as Old Believers and sectarians,” Tolstoy eventually regained his faith in God. Thence, Tolstoy developed the view that, while to rational scholars “life may be meaningless,” many people could “find meaning in life on the basis of irrational understanding or faith” (Walicki 329). In other words, Tolstoy believed that it was thanks to faith that “man does not annihilate himself” (Walicki 329). Tolstoy also noted a key distinction between theologians' faith and that of commoners (Walicki 330). Namely, he found that while the commoners understood the simple truths of life instinctively, theologian dogmas served as a means of “diverting people’s attention,” dividing them, and upholding persecution (Walicki 330).

The most notable seekers for life’s meaning in Anna Karenina are Anna and Levin. Tolstoy is most preoccupied with describing the inner turmoil (over life’s meaning) of both of these characters, which leads one to suicide and the other to a revival of sorts.

Towards the beginning of the novel, we see Anna searching for a new model of life. As she rides the train from Moscow back to St. Petersburg, she loses herself in the plot of a romance novel and wishes her life could bear much of the same passion and love. After entering a relationship with Vronsky, losing connection to Seryozha, and being chastised by society, Anna’s sole reason to live becomes Vronsky’s love. After an argument with Vronsky, she exclaims, “I want love, and it is not there. Therefore, it is all over…” (746). As her mental state deteriorates, and Anna places more doubt on Vronsky’s love for her, the narrator says that “she saw it all clearly in that penetrating light that now revealed to her the meaning of life and human relations” (765). In the scenes leading up to her suicide, Anna seemingly comes to revelations about the meaning of life, such as that “hatred and the struggle to survive are the only things that connect people” (764). However, Tolstoy makes it clear that such revelations are far from the truth (or that they, at the very least, don’t coincide with his own beliefs), for they were coming from a very mentally unstable person who was on the verge of committing suicide. There is a clear irony in Anna supposedly discovering the meaning of life right before taking her own life, suggesting that she was unable to find such meaning under the circumstances of her lifestyle.

Levin, on the other hand, finds meaning in his life in the novel’s final sequences. As in Tolstoy, philosophy or scientific analysis was insufficient as a “guide to life” for Levin (790). Due to this, as well as the realization that “all the people close to him who led good lives believed,” Levin turned to religion as a means of searching for life’s meaning (791). During Kitty’s confinement, “he, a person who did not believe, had started to pray, and in the moment when he was praying he had believed” (791). But since then, Levin struggled to maintain his faith, which led to a “painful disharmony within himself” (791). Sergey, at some point, recommends that Levin read the works of the theologian Khomyakov, which Levin initially is gratified by but eventually becomes disillusioned with because of divisions among various churches (792). It is not through “Plato, Spinoza, Kant, Schelling, Hegel, and Schopenhauer” (792) or through Khomyakov that Levin can find the meaning of life, but rather through one of his peasants, Fydor. Through this conversation, Levin has the epiphany that one must live by “obeying the truth” and “obeying God’s will” (798).

Thus, much of Levin’s spiritual journey coincides with Tolstoy’s own (after having written Anna Karenina). Both come to realize that, for them, common people have more insight into the simple truths of life than philosophers and theologians.
