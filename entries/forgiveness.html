Forgiveness

Related Articles

  • Forgiving

Forgiveness in Anna Karenina functions as a central moral concept through which Tolstoy explores the possibility, limits, and consequences of reconciliation. It can be understood as the capacity to move beyond another person’s wrongdoing and restore a relationship. However, Tolstoy does not present forgiveness as a simple moral good. Instead, he develops it as a dual phenomenon: forgiveness that is granted and forgiveness that is withheld, each producing very different outcomes in the lives of his characters.

The novel introduces forgiveness early in Part 1, Chapter 19, through the crisis in the Oblonsky household. After Stiva’s adultery, Dolly initially refuses reconciliation but ultimately forgives him following Anna’s intervention. Anna urges her to forgive if there is still love left, and Dolly’s decision reflects a kind of moral endurance rather than resolution. Anna Moreland describes this forgiveness as almost “heroic,” suggesting a specifically Christian dimension in which forgiveness exceeds ordinary human capacity. At this point, Anna herself claims she would forgive in Dolly’s position, telling her, “No, I can, I definitely can. Yes, I would forgive” (Tolstoy 73). This statement, however, later proves unstable, as Anna becomes increasingly unable to forgive Vronsky even imagined betrayals, including his contact with Princess Sorokina and his perceived emotional distance.

The most explicit and consequential act of forgiveness appears in Part 4, Chapter 17, in Karenin’s response to Anna’s affair. After her near death, he declares, “I saw her and I forgave her” (Tolstoy 417). His forgiveness extends to both Anna and Vronsky and is framed in explicitly religious terms, including the idea of turning the other cheek. Yet this act does not restore harmony. Instead, it destabilizes both Anna and Vronsky. Anna experiences Karenin’s forgiveness as a form of moral superiority that places her in an unbearable position of inferiority, while Vronsky is driven toward his failed suicide attempt. Forgiveness here is powerful but also deeply unsettling, showing that it does not necessarily heal the relationships it addresses.

A quieter form of forgiveness appears in Levin’s relationship with Kitty. In Part 4, Chapter 13, when Levin encounters Kitty again after a long separation, he tells her, “I have nothing to forgive; I have never ceased loving you” (Tolstoy 401). Unlike Karenin’s dramatic declaration, Levin’s forgiveness is implicit and grounded in continuity of feeling rather than moral judgment. A similar attitude shapes his care for his brother Nikolay, where forgiveness is expressed through empathy and presence during his final illness. In these cases, forgiveness enables reconciliation and emotional stability.

In contrast, Tolstoy also presents failures of forgiveness. In Part 1, Chapter 8, Koznyshev is unable to forgive his brother Nikolay, effectively abandoning the possibility of reconciliation when he admits that helping him is impossible (Tolstoy 29). Anna and Vronsky likewise fail to forgive one another as their relationship deteriorates. Their inability to move past suspicion and resentment leads to escalating conflict, culminating in Anna’s suicide in Part 7. Her final words, “Lord, forgive me for everything!” (Tolstoy 771), shift the question of forgiveness beyond human relationships and toward divine judgment. The novel does not resolve this question, leaving open whether Anna is ultimately forgiven.

Tolstoy’s treatment of forgiveness also engages broader intellectual and religious traditions. As Fr. Stephen Freeman summarizes a central Dostoevskian idea, one should “forgive everyone for everything,” reflecting an ideal of radical Christian forgiveness associated with The Brothers Karamazov. At the same time, this ideal is not without tension. Arthur Schopenhauer questions the idea of eternal punishment, suggesting that it is unjust to condemn someone to endless suffering for actions committed in a limited lifetime. This perspective helps illuminate Tolstoy’s own sympathy for Anna, whose actions lead to severe social and personal consequences that seem disproportionate to her desire for love. Yet Schopenhauer also warns that “to forgive and forget means to throw away a dearly bought experience,” which complicates the idea of unconditional forgiveness. In Anna Karenina, this tension is visible in the fact that forgiveness is both necessary for reconciliation and difficult to sustain in practice.

Overall, Anna Karenina presents forgiveness not as a single moral command but as a decisive force shaping human relationships. Where forgiveness is granted, it can enable continuity and reconciliation, though not without cost. Where it is withheld, relationships collapse, often irreversibly. By placing these outcomes side by side, Tolstoy suggests that forgiveness is indispensable yet unstable and that its consequences cannot be reduced to a simple moral rule.
