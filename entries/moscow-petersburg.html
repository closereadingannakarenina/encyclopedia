Moscow vs. St. Petersburg

By Simrin Madia

The setting of Leo Tolstoy’s Anna Karenina shifts between two major Russian cities, Moscow and St. Petersburg. The distinction between Moscow and St. Petersburg is not merely geographical, but conceptual, illustrating a clear civic divide within nineteenth-century Russia. The two cities functioned not only as political and cultural centers, but also as symbolic representations of competing visions of Russian civilization. Russian writers and intellectuals treated the two cities as embodiments of broader tensions between tradition and modernization, Orthodoxy and Westernization, domesticity and bureaucracy, and native Russian identity and European influence (Monas). These two cities embody competing models of Russian life in the decades following the Emancipation Reform of 1861. Tolstoy highlights the contrasts in authenticity and artificiality, tradition and modernity, and moral substance and social performance. He reflects his own values through his portrayal, acceptance, or critique of the two cultural and social environments.

In 1703, Peter the Great founded St. Petersburg as the “window to Europe.” He created a Table of Ranks, which reconfigured existing nobility into a service class. Rank and privilege, based on military achievements or bureaucratic credentials, redefined noble status. St. Petersburg functioned as the epicenter of this new reform. The city, ministries, and courts supported a strict hierarchy. Unlike Moscow’s gradual historical development, St. Petersburg was an artificial, carefully planned imperial capital. Its broad avenues, monumental architecture, and geometric layout reflected the rationalism and administrative order associated with Peter’s reforms. Russian writers frequently described the city as “intentional,” emphasizing both its modernity and its artificiality (Monas). This transformation marked a process of rapid Westernization, in which social life, government, and aristocratic culture were modeled after European nations (Morson 144–45).

As St. Petersburg rose as the center of imperial administration and Europeanized aristocratic culture, Moscow retained its older social customs, familial culture, Orthodoxy, and symbolic continuity with the old Rus’ (Monas). It provided a slower, more traditional way of life. Social status remained ingrained in old, noble families and landed estates. Even after the Emancipation Reform of 1861, Moscow and St. Petersburg elites diverged. In St. Petersburg, power came from imperial proximity, and it was standard for nobles to learn French. In Moscow, Russian traditions persisted. This divide reflects broader anxieties within Russian society about Westernization and national identity, as intellectuals struggled to reconcile European influence with native traditions (Offord 241). Moscow became associated with historical continuity and old Rus’, while St. Petersburg became associated with modernization and Russia’s turn toward Europe. By the nineteenth century, the opposition between the two cities had become one of the central symbolic structures in Russian intellectual and literary culture (Monas).

There was a fundamental disagreement between the Slavophiles and Westernizers. Slavophiles associated Moscow with Orthodoxy, communalism (sobornost), patriarchal traditions, and native Russian identity, while Westernizers viewed Petersburg as necessary for progress, modernization, rationalization, and engagement with Europe. The traditional "Slavophile," conservative mythology believed that Moscow stood as a patriarchal symbol of a way of life largely untouched by Westernization, while St. Petersburg embodied the worst aspects of Westernization. Moscow served as the cultural “heart” of Russia, while St. Petersburg stood for imposed modernization (Lieven 134).

Tolstoy shared many Slavophile critiques of Westernization and presented the two cities through this lens in War and Peace. In Anna Karenina, however, this mythology undergoes a shift that distinguishes Anna Karenina from War and Peace and much contemporary literature. Moscow is still preferable to St. Petersburg, but it too has been affected by Westernization. As seen in the novel, railways, telegrams, and urban consumer culture increasingly penetrate Moscow life, and money can be spent on entertainment with impunity, leading to financial ruin. In Anna Karenina, Tolstoy depicts the Moscow-St. Petersburg divide through the elites. In the novel, Moscow represents a semi-patriarchal, land-based aristocracy. St. Petersburg represents the Westernized imperial state, centered around bureaucracy (Lieven 134). With firsthand experience, he expresses a skeptical view of St. Petersburg. His views of St. Petersburg and Moscow are expressed through setting, character, and narrative. Plots set in St. Petersburg include government offices and social gatherings that engender surveillance and behavioral codes. Tolstoy paints this city’s social life as formal and controlled, where characters adhere to a rigid code. The contrast between Moscow and St. Petersburg reflects a deeper divide between authentic Russian values and the artificial influence of the West. As the storyline develops, Tolstoy makes clear that these settings are not passive backdrops. They actively outline the lived experiences and outcomes of the characters within them.

Anna Karenina’s husband, Alexei Karenin, is a high-ranking government official whose identity is largely shaped by his bureaucratic role. When he learns of Anna’s affair, he disregards emotion and instead prioritizes their reputation. His response reflects a mindset in which personal relationships are secondary to public perception. Anna herself is ultimately ostracized by St. Petersburg society, because she departs from its rigid norms. This response reflects the hypocrisy of St. Petersburg society, which condemns visible deviance while tolerating hidden immorality (Morson 57).

The Moscow storylines function as an alternative to this superficiality in St. Petersburg, namely through Muscovites Konstantin Levin, Kitty Shcherbatsky, and Dolly Oblonskaya. Although Levin spends most of his life on his estate, he has deep ties to Moscow from his upbringing and education. He also met the Shcherbatskys, a Muscovite family, in Moscow. Levin and Kitty’s experiences emphasize sincerity, emotional transparency, and moral development. Kitty’s emotional journey, from romantic illusion to maturity, unfolds within this environment, suggesting that Moscow permits, and even fosters, personal growth. Dolly further represents Russian tradition through her devotion to motherhood and family. Although she suffers from her husband’s infidelity, she chooses forgiveness. Tolstoy uses these characters to emphasize the Muscovite values of duty, tradition, and close involvement in family and land (Roosevelt 157–58). He does not idealize Moscow, but he admires its relative authenticity and humanity. He makes clear that Moscow remains imperfect, but prioritizes family values and human relationships.

Although Tolstoy displays Moscow in a more positive light than St. Petersburg, he nonetheless addresses Moscow’s flaws. Stiva Oblonsky’s unfaithfulness to Dolly is a recurring issue throughout the novel. Levin’s, Tolstoy’s autobiographical character, condemns this behavior. Furthermore, when Levin visits Oblonsky in Moscow, he spends excessive time at the club, gambling and drinking (Tolstoy 678–696). By comparing Moscow to Levin’s home in the countryside, Tolstoy illustrates Moscow’s shortcomings.

The contrast between Moscow and St. Petersburg in Anna Karenina reflects broader nineteenth-century tensions surrounding national identity, social change, and imperial reform. Moscow’s old, aristocratic traditions diverge from St. Petersburg’s modernity, social strata, and state bureaucracy. These tensions stemmed from mid-nineteenth-century reforms, which transformed Russia from a society structured around serfdom into one increasingly defined by civic institutions and bureaucratic governance (Lincoln 62). Tolstoy shows how these settings shape his characters’ outcomes. Anna’s tragedy is tied to the suffocating social structure of St. Petersburg. Moscow, while equally aristocratic on the surface, allows for more visible family values. However, Tolstoy’s ideal is most closely achieved in the countryside. Tolstoy remains skeptical of Westernization and suggests that true morality is only found outside the structures of both cities. The Moscow-St. Petersburg divide therefore reflects not only Tolstoy’s personal values, but also one of the defining cultural and ideological tensions of nineteenth-century Russia. In literature and intellectual thought, the two cities became symbolic representations of competing visions of Russian identity, historical development, and national destiny.
