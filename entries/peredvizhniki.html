Peredvizhniki (Realist Art)

By Harold Campusano

The Peredvizhniki is a group of Russian realist artists who challenged the restrictive character of the Russian Academy of Arts, which developed under Nicholas I (1825–1855) and widely persisted well after his reign despite reform efforts. It sought the emancipation of artists and the transformation of society through art. In Anna Karenina, the Peredvizhniki mythology is embodied by the painter Mikhailov, whom Anna and Vronsky visit during their time abroad in Italy. The novel’s scenes with Mikhailov are essential to understanding the issues pervading Tolstoy’s contemporary society.

Nicholas I believed that the Russian Academy of Arts would function much better under a more bureaucratic system, which entailed the rigorous enforcement of government regulations. Valkenier summarizes that during Nicholas I’s reign, which lasted from 1825 to 1855, “the Academy was transformed into an instrument that molded artists into servitors of the state, subordinated art to the needs and tastes of the Court and controlled artistic life throughout the country” (Valkenier 4). Nicholas I created an extensive hierarchy among graduating artists (the Amendment of 1840), as well as a system of rewarding artists for skillfully doing the government's bidding and punishing those who didn’t. Nicholas I’s successor, Alexander II, liberalized the Academy of Arts from the harsh clutches of the government, and Fedor Lvov was appointed as its Conference Secretary. While Lvov attempted to change the Academy through the reform of 1859, it widely failed to “alter the relationship of the Academy to the Court or to lessen official control over artistic activities in the country” (Valkenier 10). This led to growing tension between the Academy and artists who sought a greater role in Russian culture rather than simply carrying out the government's wishes. While many artists wished to take a stand against the Academy and join the Intelligentsia, their lack of education and economic agency made it challenging to push for change.

Finally, in 1863, fourteen contenders for the Big Gold Medal in the Academy of Arts, led by Ivan Kramskoy, refused to participate in the competition to advocate for their “individuality, self-determination, professional and creative independence” (Valkenier 33). These bold artists, with no particular goals in mind other than the aforementioned ideals, established an Artel, i.e., “a new form of communal living” (Valkenier 34). The Artel, despite being a step towards the independence of artists, was still not accepted by the Intelligentsia because of the enduring stigma against artists as well as the connection that remained between them and the Academy; namely, that the Artel relied on the Academy for “commissions, exhibitions and even studio space” (Valkenier 36).

On November 23, 1869, a group of well-known artists (e.g., Vasili Perov, Nikolai Ge) signed and sent a draft of statutes for a new organization (the Tovarishchestvo) to the Artel in St. Petersburg. Many artists in the group had their own motives, but they were united by the desire to completely sever ties with the Academy of Arts and to “widen the circle of those interested in art” (Valkenier 39). The final draft of the statutes was signed and submitted by only 4 members of the Artel to the proper authorities in September 1870. The Tovarishchestvo organized annual traveling exhibitions throughout the Russian Empire to “[acquaint] a wider public with contemporary art,” which included the Peredvizhnik exhibit that opened in St. Petersburg on November 28, 1871 (Valkenier 39–40). Despite struggles from within (multiple instances of resignation) and external pressures coming from the Academy, the Tovarishchestvo soon became a pillar of Russian culture and even an “integral part of the intelligentsia’s strivings to liberate thought and activity from the close supervision of the state” (Valkenier 40). While having successfully detached itself from the Academy, the Peredvizhniki faced many economic and censorship challenges, which meant that its artists depended heavily on wealthy donors. Much of their art depicted the landscapes, religion, and lifestyles of Russians in Moscow and St. Petersburg, which were often imbued with social criticism.

The artist, Mikhailov, whom Anna and Vronsky visit in Italy, represents the mythos of the Peredvizhniki. Golenishchev describes him as “eccentric and completely uneducated” (470), and one of the first scenes we see of him is about his landlady “demanding money” (471), and how “he never worked more ardently and productively than when his life was going badly” (471). Immediately, then, we know that Mikhailov is uneducated, lacking sufficient funds to comfortably sustain himself, and yet still a very passionate artist—much like members of the Peredvizhniki.

Alexandrov, quite convincingly, argues that the passages with the artist whom Anna and Vronsky visit in Italy, Mikhailov, represent the “difficulty if not the impossibility of communicating a spiritual reality via an artistic medium” (Alexandrov 84). This is especially apparent through Vronsky’s observations on Mikhailov’s The Admonition of Pilate, in which he highlights his amazement at Mikhailov’s “technique” (476). Mikhailov is “grated painfully” by this remark because, like many members of the Peredvizhniki, Mikhailov does not view being a true artist as merely having the “mechanical ability” to create something beautiful (476), which coincides with the Peredvizhniki’s rejection of the government’s emphasis on “training technicians who could fulfill Court commissions with skill and dispatch” (Valkenier 6).
